Case Study — 01

DealFinder

Grocery planning for budget-conscious students

University students living independently struggle with three overlapping problems: rising grocery costs, overwhelming recipe choices, and the time it takes to cross-reference deals across multiple platforms. DealFinder is a mobile app concept that consolidates grocery deals, recipe discovery, and smart list-building into a single, intuitive experience — designed entirely through a five-stage design thinking process with real user testing at every iteration.

01 — 02

Empathize & Define

The Problem Space

Cooking, meal planning, and grocery management are genuinely hard for students living on their own for the first time. Through interviews and surveys, we uncovered a layered problem — it wasn't just one thing, it was the collision of budget pressure, time scarcity, and decision fatigue hitting simultaneously.

Key Pain Points Identified

  • Buying small portions is difficult, leading to food waste
  • Rising grocery costs make it hard to find the best deals
  • Too many recipe options feel overwhelming rather than helpful
  • Planning meals across multiple apps and stores is too time-consuming
Refined Design Question

"How might we make it more cost effective and time efficient for university students to plan meals and source relevant grocery deals?"

User Story

"As a university student, I want to efficiently find deals and recipes I can make cheaply so I can save time and money."

03

Ideate

Bulk Ideation

Each team member independently generated ideas against the design question. We ran affinity diagramming sessions to surface categories — and discovered a surprising new cluster: community-oriented solutions (shared lists, peer recipe sharing) that hadn't come up in earlier sessions.

Analogous Inspiration

  • Uber Eats — intuitive browsing of many options without feeling lost
  • Spotify/Netflix — personalised recommendations that feel curated to you
  • Tinder Swiping — fast, low-effort triage of suggestions
  • Stock Market — historical price visibility to spot the best deals

Ideation Categories

  • Deal / coupon consolidation
  • Generative recipe suggestion
  • Smart list building
  • Community-driven services
  • Grocery store finding
  • Budget tracking
  • Generative meal planning

04 — 05

Prototype & Test

Phase 1 — Paper Prototyping

Free-wheeling and individual — each team member sketched their own design vision with varying levels of fidelity. The goal was quantity over quality: surface as many ideas as possible, then identify what the best elements were across all versions.

This produced general structural concepts: recipe generator, search by food group, store preferences, budget setting. As iterations continued, the level of detail increased naturally.

Phase 2 — Figma Prototyping

Designs consolidated into Figma — moving from multiple individual visions to a single shared prototype. High-level design decisions were confirmed, page-to-page connections were mapped, and all major structural choices were locked in.

At this stage, key simplifications emerged — for instance, merging "Browse by Food Group" and "Browse by Top Deals" into a single tabbed view after user feedback revealed the distinction was confusing.

REFLECTION

What I Learned

What Worked Well

  • The big-picture ⇌ small-picture design thinking cycles kept us from getting lost in details too early
  • Analogous inspiration (Uber Eats, Spotify) gave us strong mental models to build on rather than starting from zero
  • Iterative user testing caught usability issues we genuinely hadn't anticipated
  • Freely cycling between ingredient and recipe browsing emerged as a key differentiator from existing apps

What I'd Do Differently

  • Get user feedback earlier — tangible interfaces triggered insights that early research didn't surface
  • Lock the design question before starting ideation — the first session was constrained by the original (too narrow) question
  • Build a functional prototype for more realistic usability testing beyond clickable Figma flows
  • Explore community-oriented features more deeply — they emerged late but showed real potential

Next Steps

Develop a functional prototype for iterative user testing across multiple design-test-build-learn cycles. Explore social recipe sharing between friends, improved deal-to-recipe matching algorithms, and a home screen widget for the generated grocery list to support in-store use.
